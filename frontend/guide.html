How the Atlas is organised, what the categories mean, how convergence rate (δ) is measured, and how to verify path-independence for 2D and 3D CMFs.

1 · CMF Categories

Every entry in the Atlas is classified into one of three categories. The classification is automatic and based on dimension, polynomial degree, and whether the limit has been identified as a known transcendental constant.

Reference

Trivial / Rational

Small 2D CMFs (degree ≤ 1, or degree ≤ 2 with no identified transcendental constant). The limit is either rational, unknown, or uninteresting. These serve as a calibration baseline for walk mechanics and convergence testing.

Examples: linear polynomials f = x+y, f = 2x−y, simple gauge families.
Interesting

Known or Irrational Limit

2D CMFs that converge to a known transcendental constant — ζ(3), π, ln(2), Catalan's G, digamma ψ(p/q), etc. — or to an unknown but possibly irrational value. These are the core mathematical objects in the Atlas.

Examples: Apéry-like CMFs (ζ(3)), degree-3 families related to ζ(5), digamma families.
Discovery

Higher-Dimensional / Ramanujan

CMFs of dimension ≥ 3, Ramanujan-style pFq hypergeometric families, and A+ manually verified entries. These represent the frontier of the CMF program and encode the deepest mathematical structure.

Examples: 3D CMF Hunter entries, 5D RamanujanTools entries, ζ(2)/π² CMFs.
Filtering: Use the Category dropdown on the Browse page to restrict results to a single category. The category is also shown as a coloured badge on every row.

2 · Self-Delta (δ) — Convergence Rate

The self-delta (δ) measures how many bits of new precision the walk gains per matrix step. It is computed from three equally-spaced points along the walk:

\[ \delta \;=\; \frac{\log_2\!\bigl(|v_{N/3} - v_N|\bigr) \;-\; \log_2\!\bigl(|v_{2N/3} - v_N|\bigr)}{N/3} \quad \text{bits per step} \]

where \(v_k\) is the partial convergent at step \(k\) and \(v_N\) is the last value (used as a proxy for the true limit).

δ ≥ 1.0
🟢 Excellent — doubling precision each step
δ ≥ 0.3
🟡 Good — ~1 digit per few steps
δ ≥ 0.05
🟠 Moderate — slow linear
δ < 0.05
🔴 Slow — likely not converging

δ is displayed in the Explorer after every walk, with a quality indicator and a link back to this page. It complements the raw convergence rate (log₁₀ scale per step) already reported.

Note: δ uses the final convergent as an approximation to the true limit. For CMFs with very slow convergence (< 100 steps), δ may be noisy. Always run depth ≥ 200 for a reliable estimate.

3 · Path-Independence

A Continued Matrix Fraction (CMF) is called flat (or path-independent) if the limit of the matrix walk does not depend on which path through the integer lattice \(\mathbb{Z}^d\) is taken. Formally, for 2D CMFs the flatness condition requires:

\[ K_x(k,\,m) \cdot K_y(k+1,\,m) \;=\; K_y(k,\,m) \cdot K_x(k,\,m+1) \]

This is the matrix commutativity condition on every lattice square. It guarantees that both the zig-zag path (x first, then y) and the alternate path (y first, then x) produce the same matrix product — and hence the same convergent.

2D CMFs — Conservative Test

For 2D CMFs with an explicit polynomial form f(x,y), the Conservative Test page evaluates the residual matrix

\[ R(k,m) \;=\; K_x(k,m)\,K_y(k{+}1,m) \;-\; K_y(k,m)\,K_x(k,m{+}1) \]

at every lattice point in a grid. For a flat CMF, every entry of \(R(k,m)\) is identically zero. The test reports the maximum Frobenius norm across the grid — a value below 10⁻¹² constitutes strong numerical evidence of flatness.

3D CMFs — Why the Matrix Test is Limited

Important: 3D CMFs in the Atlas (CMF Hunter / RamanujanTools) encode their matrix entries through a higher-level operator representation, not directly through a single polynomial f(x,y,z) that can be evaluated pointwise. The polynomial form shown in the Atlas is a projection — the full 3D matrix stack lives in a different internal representation.

This means the matrix commutativity test cannot be run directly from the polynomial form for 3D entries. The Conservative Test page will return a non-zero residual, but this does not mean the CMF is not flat — it reflects the mismatch between the projected polynomial and the full 3D operator.

3D Path-Independence: What Actually Works

The correct way to verify path-independence for 3D CMFs is to observe that multiple trajectories with different shifts converge to the same limit. In the 2D telescope walk \(K_x(k, m_0, n_0)\) with different choices of the fixed coordinates \(m_0, n_0\), a flat CMF always yields the same limit regardless of the shift. This is the defining property of flatness, directly observable without any matrix algebra.

5 · Walk Mechanics

Each trajectory accumulates a matrix product along a 1D axis of the lattice:

\[ P_N \;=\; K(s_0)\cdot K(s_0+1)\cdots K(s_0+N-1) \]

The convergent at step \(N\) is read as \(P_N[0,1] / P_N[1,1]\). For 2D CMFs, \(K = K_x(k, m_\text{fixed})\) or \(K = K_y(k_\text{fixed}, m)\). For 3D CMFs, a third direction \(K_z\) is also available.

The matrix entries of \(K_x(k, m)\) are computed from the telescope polynomial:

\[ b(k,m) \;=\; f(k,m)\cdot\bar{f}(k,m), \qquad K_x(k,m) \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -b(k,m) \\ 1 & a(k,m) \end{pmatrix} \]

where \(a(k,m) = f(k,m+1)\cdot\bar{f}(k,m) + f(k,m)\cdot\bar{f}(k,m+1)\) is the trace term derived from the conjugacy polynomial pair \((f, \bar{f})\).

Precision is maintained at 30 decimal digits (mpmath). The product is normalised every 20 steps to prevent overflow. The self-delta δ and convergence rate are computed from the final 20 convergents.
